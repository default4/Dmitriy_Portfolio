Hypothesis Testing with Insurance Data

A statistical analysis project that aims to understand the relationships between various factors and insurance charges using hypothesis testing.

PROJECTS

Hypothesis Testing with Insurance Data

April 10, 2017

Goals

  1. Understand if there’s a significant difference in insurance charges based on: Smoking status, Gender, Region.
  2. Explore the association between: Gender and smoking status, Region and smoking status.

Datasets

The dataset contains following columns:

  1. age: Age of the primary beneficiary. Ranges from 18 to 64 with an average age of approximately 39.
  2. sex: Gender of the primary beneficiary. There are slightly more males (676) than females (662) in the dataset.
  3. bmi: Body Mass Index (BMI) of the primary beneficiary. Ranges from 15.96 to 53.13 with an average BMI of approximately 30.66.
  4. children: Number of children covered by the insurance. Most individuals have 0 to 2 children, with a maximum of 5 children.
  5. smoker: Whether the primary beneficiary is a smoker or not. A majority (1064) are non-smokers, while 274 are smokers.
  6. region: Region where the primary beneficiary lives. The ‘southeast’ region has the most entries (364), but the distribution seems relatively balanced across regions.
  7. charges: Insurance charges or costs. The insurance charges range from 1121.87 to 63770.43, with an average charge of approximately 13270.42.

Following types of tests will be considered:

  1. Two Sample Tests: Compare insurance charges between smokers and non-smokers. Compare insurance charges between males and females.
  2. Multiple Sample Tests (ANOVA): Compare insurance charges across different regions.
  3. Non-Numeric Tests (Chi-Squared Test): Test the association between gender and smoking status. Test the association between region and smoking status.

Two Sample Tests

Compare insurance charges between smokers and non-smokers

Box Plot:

  1. Smokers tend to have higher insurance charges than non-smokers. The median charge for smokers is substantially higher than for non-smokers.
  2. The interquartile range (IQR) for smokers is also larger, indicating more variability in charges for smokers.

Histogram:

  1. The distribution of charges for non-smokers is skewed to the right, with most non-smokers having charges below $10,000.
  2. Smokers have a broader distribution, with a significant number of them having charges over $30,000.

It will be conducted a t-test to see if there’s a statistically significant difference in insurance charges between males and females.

Results for Charges between Smokers and Non-Smokers:

  1. T-statistic: 46.66
  2. P-value: 8.27×10^−283

Given the extremely low p-value, we can reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is a statistically significant difference in insurance charges between smokers and non-smokers.

Compare insurance charges between males and females:

Box Plot:

  1. The distribution of charges for both males and females is right-skewed.
  2. The median charge for males appears slightly higher than for females, but the difference is not as pronounced as it was between smokers and non-smokers.

Histogram:

  1. Both males and females show a right-skewed distribution for insurance charges.
  2. The distributions are relatively similar, with both genders having most of their charges below $10,000.

Next, will be conducted a t-test to see if there’s a statistically significant difference in insurance charges between males and females.

Results for Charges between Males and Females:

  1. T-statistic: 2.10
  2. P-value: 0.0361

The p-value is less than 0.05, which indicates that there’s a statistically significant difference in insurance charges between males and females, though the difference is not as pronounced as it was between smokers and non-smokers.

Multiple Sample Tests (ANOVA)

Compare insurance charges across different regions

Box Plot:

  1. The median charges appear highest for the Southeast region and lowest for the Southwest region.
  2. The Southeast region also has a wider interquartile range, suggesting more variability in charges.
  3. All regions have several outliers on the higher end of charges.

Histogram:

  1. All regions show a right-skewed distribution for insurance charges.
  2. The Southeast region has a notable peak in the higher charge range, which aligns with its higher median in the box plot.

It will be used one-way ANOVA to test if there’s a statistically significant difference in insurance charges across the different regions.

Results for Charges across Different Regions:

  1. F-statistic: 2.97
  2. P-value: 0.0309

Given the p-value is less than 0.05, we can reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is a statistically significant difference in insurance charges across the different regions.

Non-Numeric Tests (Chi-Squared Test)

Test the association between gender and smoking status

Bar Plot:

  1. Among females, a majority are non-smokers with a smaller proportion being smokers.
  2. Similarly, among males, a majority are non-smokers, but the proportion of male smokers is slightly higher than that of female smokers.

Contingency Table:

  1. Out of 662 females, 547 are non-smokers and 115 are smokers.
  2. Out of 676 males, 517 are non-smokers and 159 are smokers.

It will be used the Chi-squared test to see if there’s an association between an individual’s gender and their smoking status.

Results for Association between Gender and Smoking Status:

  1. Chi-squared statistic: 7.39
  2. P-value: 0.0065

Given the p-value is less than 0.05, we can reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is a statistically significant association between an individual’s gender and their smoking status.

Test the association between region and smoking status

Bar Plot:

  1. In all regions, the number of non-smokers is higher than smokers.
  2. The Southeast region has a relatively higher proportion of smokers compared to the other regions.

Contingency Table:

  1. In the Northeast region, out of 324 individuals, 257 are non-smokers and 67 are smokers.
  2. In the Northwest region, out of 325 individuals, 267 are non-smokers and 58 are smokers.
  3. In the Southeast region, out of 364 individuals, 273 are non-smokers and 91 are smokers.
  4. In the Southwest region, out of 325 individuals, 267 are non-smokers and 58 are smokers.

From the Chi-Squared Test results, the p-value was 0.0617. This is slightly above the common significance level of 0.05, indicating that we do not have enough evidence to suggest a statistically significant association between region and smoking status, based on our dataset.

Result

In this project, it was delved into an insurance dataset to explore relationships between various factors and insurance charges using statistical hypothesis testing. The dataset featured information about policyholders’ age, gender, BMI, number of children, smoker status, region, and their respective insurance charges.

Primary objectives were to determine:

  1. The impact of smoking status on insurance charges.
  2. The difference in insurance charges based on gender.
  3. Variations in insurance charges across different regions.
  4. The potential associations between gender and smoking status, and between region and smoking status.

Key Findings:

  1. Smokers vs. Non-Smokers: A pronounced difference in insurance charges was observed, with smokers having significantly higher charges.
  2. Males vs. Females: A difference was identified, with males having slightly higher charges than females.
  3. Regional Differences: There was a statistically significant variation in insurance charges across regions, with the Southeast region having the highest average charges.
  4. Associations: A significant association was found between gender and smoking status, suggesting gender might influence smoking habits. However, no significant association was determined between region and smoking status.
